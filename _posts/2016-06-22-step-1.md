In my last post I discussed my idea for a context aware chat bot. Some may argue knowing the product you will produce before identifying the pains it may solve, is a bit like a "solution seeking a problem" or "putting the cart before the horse". However, I do not believe it is unusual to at least know the type of product before you dig in; without some constraints the unlimited possibilities would make it unlikely that I would make forward progress on solving any problem. Even with the constraints I established in my last post, you will see in this post, there are a near infinite number of ways a context-aware bot could be applied.

I am excited that the context problem of computing has not been effectively solved. As I described in my last post, technology is great at providing you facts, but not great about providing a judgment of those facts. To render a judgment (e.g. long or short, big or small, etc.) requires context. This problem is pervasive, but to me it is most obviously lacking in the recent advent of conversational interfaces. 

While many of the fields that contribute to product management are fairly mature (e.g. HCI, project management), product management itself is still an emerging field lacking well defined boundaries and best practices. I was originally concerned about the ambiguity: how can I demonstrate excellence as a product manager without completing every step in a process? I asked others in the field, and read extensively to find the answer. Ultimately, winning at product management means knowing your goal, and deploying "right tools at the right time".

# Defining a singular goal
So, what is my goal for this product? If I could pick just one, would it be: number of users? engagement? influence? profitability? I'm building this product personally, and want to prove my abilities as a product manager, so I'm going for engagement. I believe a focus on building an engaging product is most likely to result in a product representative of the type of product manager I want to be long term. I would rather have 10 passionate users who use the product daily, than 100 who use it less than once a month (though 100 passionate users would be pretty awesome).

# Using the goal to define user focus
Since I am targeting engagement over volume, I can focus on meeting the needs of a specialized segment rather than trying to meet the needs of the greatest number of people. Not only does this align with my engagement goal, but it is also necessary given that I am a less-than-part-time-team-of-one. If I can solve one problem so exceptionally well that I get a few people exceptionally engaged in using the product, I will have met my goal.

# Evaluating Constraints & Assumptions
At this point I'm enjoying constraints, as they bring some structure to what originally seemed like unstructured work of a product manager. Before I go further, I need to document my constraints and their embedded assumptions.

| Constraint | Embedded Assumption | Validate?
|:-----------|:-----------------|:----------:|
| Specialized user segment | Context-aware product would work for a specialist | No |
| Users in North America speaking US English | I will be more successful by going with people I can meet in person and speak to | No |
| Text-based chat bot | Some interactions are best served with a text interface | Yes |
| | Targeted users can access / use web-based bot | Yes |
| | Targeted users want a bot | Yes |
| Context focused | People want context in their computer interactions | Yes |

# Solving Problems
For people to use a new product, it must address a problem or pain they experience. Even aspirational products address the problem of the person not yet being what they aspire to be. As I'm a product team of one at this point, I don't have a diverse group with varying life experiences from which to discover unaddressed problems. The unfortunate result is that I'm most likely to solve a problem I experience personally, or at least one I have observed. I wont even pretend to have a broad, worldly, perspective to draw on, so I realize I'm not seeing the majority of problems in the world worth solving. [Hinten Shah](http://women2.com/2013/12/04/create-products-people-love-validating-idea-first/) appropriately warns: "This situation is most common when people decide to solve a problem that they have themselves. You have to make sure enough other people have the problem too." So I will need to validate the assumption that a significant population experiences the problem I'm solving and would benefit from the way I solve it.

| Constraint | Embedded Assumption | Validate?
|:-----------|:------------------|:----------:|
| >1000 people experience the problem being solved | A significant population experiences the problem I'm solving and would benefit from the way I solve it | Yes |
{: rules="groups"}

# But What Problem?
In the post I linked to earlier, Hinten Shah advises you to describe your potential problem like '[Group of people] have a problem [their problem]'. This approach lends itself to discovering what the user is ultimately trying to accomplish. I won't pain you with every item that came to mind, but here are a few:

AT WORK: Knowledge Workers

* [Knowledge Workers] have a problem [that it is difficult to find the information they are looking for]
* **[Knowledge Workers] have a problem [that it take too long to teach others what they have done]**
* [Knowledge Workers] have a problem [that meetings and conference calls are not productive relative to the time they consume]
* [Team leaders] have a problem [that it is difficult to keep everyone on the team in sync about work, process, and new information]
* [Team leaders] have a problem [that team members have specialized knowledge that is not shared]
* [Decision Makers] have a problem [that it is too time consuming to instruct others in what they need to make their decision]
* [Knowledge Workers] have a problem [that it is difficult to decide what to do next]
* [Team leaders] have a problem [that it is difficult to allocate work between team members fairly]
* [Knowledge Workers] have a problem [that it is time consuming to deal with different granularity of data]
* [Knowledge Workers] have a problem [that it is time consuming to find simple answers]
* **[Process Leaders] have a problem [that it is difficult to manage complex integrated system/people processes]**
* **[Project Managers] have a problem [that it is time consuming to collect updates and report status]**
* [Knowledge Workers] have a problem [that data lineage is difficult to use]

AT WORK: Developers

* [Bot Developers] have a problem [that they do not know how to obtain and process a user's context]

AT WORK: Businesses

* [Business Owners] have a problem [that it is difficult to provide the right information to customers (or potential customers) at the right time]

AT HOME

* [Online shoppers] have a problem [that checkout requires too much data entry]
* [Home owners] have a problem [that they do not know what is causing their energy bills to be so high]
* [Multi-member households] have a problem [that individuals do not know where someone else put something away]
* [Home owners] have a problem [that they don't remember when they need to do specific maintenance on their home]

\\


While my experience informing these problem statements, I notice two obvious themes:

1. Status updates are a problem, whether it is a project manager, leader, or even conference call participant. 
2. Finding the right information at the right time, is a challenge affecting everyone.

---

I did not expect these two to emerge. Interestingly, both can be addressed with bot technology, but is that really the best way to solve the problem? *This is what I need to validate.*
