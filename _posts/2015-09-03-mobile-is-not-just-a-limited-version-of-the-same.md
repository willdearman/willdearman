Often the way we succeed in new environments is to apply mental models we've previously developed. Businesses do this too. It works well in allowing us to adapt quickly, except when it doesn't. Mobile devices and services are one area where previous models did not hold well, and I believe the 'invisible interfaces' of the future will be another.

> "We've always thought of 'mobile internet' as a cut-down subset of the 'real' Internet. Time to invert that. It's the desktop that's limited"
> - [Benedict Evans](https://twitter.com/BenedictEvans/status/637660040308985856)

But the limitation is more than just the desktop interface to the internet being more limited. We have different use cases, preferred interactions, and applications of mobile than we do desktops. Mobile fills a different, active, part of our lives where mental models around desktop interactions don't work. Ask yourself why apps and notifications have become the dominant model for mobile? It is about what we are trying to do, rather than how we do it, that matters on mobile.

I also believe our mental models about the internet are also rapidly becoming outdated. We don't use the internet to view web pages, download songs, or communicate in social networks. We use the internet to learn, listen, and share. That is a hard mental model to break.

> "One thing you can do is think, I think, we’ll have to see how this plays out – people who are 8 or 9 right now, the new wave are brought into the internet world with mobile. There’ll be a huge distinction between me and them. Culturally it’ll be completely different. Their first interaction they had was with mobile." 
> - [Soroush Ghodsi](http://thenextweb.com/entrepreneur/2015/08/29/seriously-watch-out-zuck/)

But consider how you would view the world, if you had no concept of archaic desktop applications or a file-based internet. If your baseline was mobile's intention-based view. What would your next step be?
