"I need a career change," I found myself thinking several months ago; not a new job but a different career path. While my career is great and still accelerating, I realize my current path will never provide some of the creative fulfillment I crave. It was an interesting journey finding the insight that a career in product management could potentially provide the analytical + creative challenges plus other things I'm looking for, but I'll save that for a future post. This post is about my first steps towards product management. 

When I began exploring the day-to-day work of product managers, I realized a surprising amount of overlap in the skills required to be a successful product manager, and the skills that have made me successful in my career in bank regulatory reporting. I then further realized I am actually a product manager of sorts, with my regulatory data being the product. It sounds like a stretch, but: 

- ["A PM is responsible for making sure that a team ships a great product."](https://kindle.amazon.com/work/cracking-pm-interview-product-technology-ebook/B00H3R1N9U/B00ISYMUR6) - In my job I am responsible for ensuring my team delivers an accurate and timely report that reconciles with other data sources. If there is a problem with our process, it becomes my responsibility. If there is a problem with our reporting, I am accountable.
- ["You own the vision, strategy, and roadmap."](https://medium.com/swlh/the-role-of-a-product-manager-e0354b4b6845) - I've got that too. The vision (albeit boring) is a timely and accurate report. The strategy is one where we employ a dash of data management, a spoonful of risk controls, and a pinch of manual validation, among other things. Our roadmap is one where we evolve features of technology platforms to reduce manual adjustments or improve the efficiency of our process.
- ["Once he’s chosen a feature set, the product manager becomes the expert on them. He’ll think deeply about the problems he’s trying to solve and the goals of the features. In the coming phases, everyone on the team will have questions, including “why are we working on this?”, and the PM will need to have answers."](https://kindle.amazon.com/work/cracking-pm-interview-product-technology-ebook/B00H3R1N9U/B00ISYMUR6) - I define the production plan, timeline, and controls. I define how we will work with other bank functions. Ultimately I can justify every action our team takes either by regulatory mandate, or business case.
- Soft skills of influencing diverse stakeholders - I've got that too. Getting that difficult capital markets finance MD on the same page as the business analyst while navigating inconsistent regulatory instructions: check.
- Jobs to Be Done framework - My most successful project plans have been ones where I discarded the unnecessarily sequential tasks lists in favor of systems of processes around specific tangible deliverables.

I could go on, but needless to say there are many instances. Which is swell, but not something a sane person would realize within [5 to 7 seconds](http://theundercoverrecruiter.com/infographic-recruiters-spend-5-7-seconds-reading-your-cv/) of seeing my profile. So, how can I prove that I have the potential to be an exceptional product manager in a technology-oriented company?

Build a product, and write about my process!
